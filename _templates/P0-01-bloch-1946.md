# P0-01 · Nuclear Induction — Bloch (1946)

> **One-line summary**: describes the precession and relaxation of the magnetization vector using classical (not quantum) physics, establishing the physical basis of all NMR/MR signal.

## Core content

Bloch aggregates the magnetic moments of all hydrogen protons in a sample into one macroscopic **magnetization vector M**, and writes its time evolution:

$$\frac{d\vec M}{dt} = \gamma\,\vec M \times \vec B \;-\; \frac{M_x\hat i + M_y\hat j}{T_2} \;-\; \frac{(M_z - M_0)\hat k}{T_1}$$

- First term: **precession**. M precesses around B at the Larmor frequency $\omega_0 = \gamma B_0$ — the "heartbeat" of the MR signal.
- Second term: **transverse relaxation T2**. Mxy decays exponentially, corresponding to signal loss (dephasing).
- Third term: **longitudinal relaxation T1**. Mz slowly returns to the equilibrium value M0.

The signal measured by the coil (the FID) is the EMF induced by the transverse component Mxy.

## Key understanding

| Concept | Physical meaning | Role in MRI |
|---------|------------------|-------------|
| Precession | M rotates around B | Determines resonance frequency; origin of frequency encoding |
| T1 | Longitudinal recovery | One tissue-contrast parameter; one of the two main MRF fingerprint parameters |
| T2 | Transient decay | The other tissue-contrast parameter; the other main MRF fingerprint parameter |

## Implications for my research

**MRF is essentially repeatedly "kicking" this M vector and then watching how it relaxes back.** Dictionary simulation in MRF is, at its core, the numerical integration of the Bloch equation (or its steady-state generalization). So:

- To truly understand the signal evolution of FISP/bSSFP-MRF, you must return to the transient / steady-state solutions of the Bloch equation.
- In my non-Cartesian MRF work, the K-space trajectory decides "how to sample", while the Bloch equation decides "what is being sampled" — the two are decoupled, but both are indispensable.

## Writing lessons (from Bloch)

1. **Dimensional reduction in presentation**: he deliberately avoids quantum mechanics and turns NMR into a classical electromagnetism problem, letting a wide audience of physicists/engineers pick it up instantly — **top papers often make hard problems simple**.
2. **One equation rules the whole paper**: the entire paper is organized around a single vector equation, giving it a razor-sharp structure.
3. **Experiment corroborates theory**: water + a detection coil immediately verifies the observable signal — theory and experiment form a closed loop.
